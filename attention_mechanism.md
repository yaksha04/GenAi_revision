Before attention came along, models (like standard RNNs) tried to "remember" an entire sentence by squashing it into a single mathematical vector. Imagine trying to summarize a 500-page novel into one paragraph—you’re bound to lose the fine details.Here is how that "simple idea" actually functions in practice:1. The Search Engine Logic (Query, Key, Value)To decide what to focus on, the mechanism uses a system similar to a database search:Query ($Q$): What I am currently looking for (the current word).Key ($K$): What information is available (the "tags" of all other words).Value ($V$): The actual information content of those words.The model calculates a score by comparing the Query with all Keys. If the query "bank" matches the key "money" more than the key "river," the model assigns a higher weight to the "money" value.2. The Weighting ProcessInstead of looking at everything equally, the model creates a probability distribution (using the Softmax function).Word A: 85% focusWord B: 10% focusWord C: 5% focusThis means when the model is predicting the next word, it literally "ignores" the noise and amplifies the signal from the most relevant context.3. Why it Changed EverythingParallelization: Unlike older models that had to read word-by-word (linearly), attention allows the model to look at the entire sequence at once.Long-Range Dependencies: It solves the "forgetting" problem. In a sentence like "The man who lived in the house across the street from the park... was tall," attention allows the model to link "man" directly to "was" without getting lost in the middle.
